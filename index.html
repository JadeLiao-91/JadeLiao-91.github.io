01 — About Who, what, why
"To be chary of the easy answer is to take thinking seriously."

I was born in Quanzhou, Fujian — the place Tieguanyin tea comes from — and grew up in Guangzhou, where I spent a lot of time in teahouses listening to grown-ups argue. I'm now a junior at HFI, in the Ainsworth class.

My two favorite classes look like opposites but feel the same to me. In AP World History with Mr. Schneider, we look at texts and patterns to figure out how things change (and don't change) over time. In AP Physics C with Dr. Ma, we do the same kind of thing — just with equations. Both classes are basically asking: what stays the same across different cases?

In AP Lang, Dr. Li taught us about System 1 and System 2 thinking, and it stuck. I notice it in myself: in Chinese, I just say what I think. In English, I run it past myself two or three times first.

I'm still working out which version of me I trust more.

06 — Personal narrative

On thinking in two languages

I grew up speaking Cantonese at home and Mandarin at school, drinking tea poured by both my grandfathers. Guangzhou is the kind of city where people still go to teahouses to argue about politics, property, anything. I didn't really "learn" Chinese. It was just how we talked.

English came later — first as a school subject, then as the language of pretty much everything I do at HFI. I noticed something almost right away: I sound different in English. Slower. More careful. In Chinese, I'll just say what I'm thinking. In English, I run it past myself two or three times before I open my mouth.

I thought this was just a me thing. Then I read Keysar, Hayakawa, and An (2012). They found that bilingual people make less biased decisions in their second language — not because it makes you smarter, but because it carries less emotional weight. Your first language is the language of your family, your jokes, the things that embarrass you. A second language doesn't have all that. So the version of me that speaks English is more careful. The version of me that speaks Chinese is more... actually me, just unfiltered.

That kind of bothers me. Which version do I trust? Which one is me?

I haven't figured it out yet. But here's where I am right now — I don't think I have to pick. Quanzhou is where my family is from. Guangzhou is where I grew up. HFI is where I'm learning to think in a second language, and learning that thinking itself has a language.

I'm trying to be the person who can sit in all three rooms.
